    Energy Requirements and Diet

    Key Concepts

    Energy Balance and Weight

    A balanced diet provides the correct amount of energy and nutrients for health. The relationship between energy intake and body weight is governed by the principle of energy balance:

    • Energy Surplus: If Energy In (Food) > Energy Out (Activity + Basic Metabolism), the body stores the excess energy as fat, leading to weight gain.
    • Energy Deficit: If Energy In < Energy Out, the body uses stored fat for energy, leading to weight loss and becoming thinner.

    Factors Affecting Energy Requirements

    Not everyone needs the same amount of energy. Requirements vary based on:

    • Age: Children and teenagers need energy for growth and development.
    • Activity Level: Physical activity burns energy. Sedentary people need less than active people.
    • Physiological State: Pregnant women need extra energy for the growth of the fetus.

    Data Analysis: Graph C

    The bar chart shows how average energy requirements change with age and activity.

    • Trend: Energy needs generally increase from age 5 to adulthood.
    • Activity Impact: "Very active adults" require significantly more energy (~15,000 kJ) compared to standard adults (~10,000 kJ).

    Review Questions and Answers

    Question 3

    a) Suggest why a teenager needs more energy than a 5-year-old child.

    • Growth: Teenagers undergo rapid growth spurts (puberty), requiring significant energy to build new tissue and bone.
    • Body Size: A teenager has a larger body mass than a 5-year-old, requiring more energy for basic maintenance (metabolism).
    • Activity: Teenagers are often more physically active than young children.

    b) Why do you think a pregnant woman needs more energy from food than a woman who is not pregnant?

    • Energy is required for the growth and development of the fetus (baby).
    • Energy is needed for the growth of the placenta and the enlargement of the uterus and breasts.

    Question 4

    a) Write down these people in order of who needs the least energy to the most energy: baby, fire-fighter, secretary, 11-year-old child.

    Order: Baby < 11-year-old child < Secretary < Fire-fighter

    b) Explain your answer to part a.

    • Baby: Has the lowest total energy requirement because they have a very small body mass and spend most of their time sleeping (low activity).
    • 11-year-old child: Needs more than a baby due to growth and higher activity levels (playing/school), but less than an adult due to smaller body size (See Graph C: Age 11 is lower than Adult).
    • Secretary: An adult has a larger body mass than a child (requiring more maintenance energy). Although their job is sedentary, their baseline metabolic need is higher than the child's.
    • Fire-fighter: Requires the most energy because they are an adult (large mass) plus they perform very heavy physical labor (high activity), similar to the "Very active adult" in Graph C.

    Question 5

    A 5-year-old only eats buns.

    a) How much would he have to eat each day to get the energy he needs?

    From Graph C, a 5-year-old needs approximately 6,000 kJ (reading the bar between 4000 and 8000).

    Using data from the previous page (Bun = 544 kJ):

    $$ \text{Number of buns} = \frac{6000}{544} \approx 11 \text{ buns} $$

    Answer: He would need to eat approximately 11 buns per day.

    b) If he only ate falafel burgers, how much would he have to eat each day?

    From previous page data (Falafel burger = 1990 kJ):

    $$ \text{Number of burgers} = \frac{6000}{1990} \approx 3 \text{ burgers} $$

    Answer: He would need to eat approximately 3 burgers per day.

    c) Why shouldn't you always eat only one type of food?

    • Nutrient Deficiency: One type of food rarely contains all the essential nutrients (vitamins, minerals, protein, fibre) needed for health.
    • Health Issues: Eating only buns (carbohydrates/fats) would lead to deficiencies (e.g., Scurvy from lack of Vitamin C) and poor growth. A balanced diet requires a mixture of different foods.

    Question 6

    Explain the link between the amount of food someone eats, the amount of activity they carry out and the amount of weight they gain.

    Weight gain occurs when there is an energy imbalance.

    • If a person eats a large amount of food (High Energy In) but carries out very little activity (Low Energy Out), the excess energy is stored as fat.
    • Conversely, high activity levels burn more energy, reducing the likelihood of weight gain even if food intake is moderate.

    Question 7

    Look at photo E. How will the amount of time koalas spend asleep affect the amount of food they need to find?

    • Koalas feed on eucalyptus leaves, which provide very low energy (as stated in the Fact box).
    • By sleeping for 20 hours a day, koalas minimize their energy expenditure (activity).
    • This allows them to survive on a low-energy diet because they are conserving energy rather than burning it through movement.

    Question 8

    Scientists can measure the amount of energy stored in different foods. How can this knowledge help mountain climbers and explorers?

    • Calorimetry: Scientists use calorimetry (burning food to heat water) to measure energy content (kJ).
    • Application: Mountain climbers (like those in Photo D) have extremely high energy needs (~19,000 kJ/day) but can only carry limited weight.
    • Knowing the exact energy values allows them to pack energy-dense foods (foods with high kJ per gram), ensuring they have enough fuel for the climb without carrying excessive bulk.

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this topic, you should be able to:

    • Recall that our bodies need energy, which we get from food
    • Explain why different people need different amounts of energy from food (Age, Activity, Growth)
    • Recall that the units for measuring energy are joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ), where 1 kJ = 1000 J
    • Explain the link between diet, activity, and weight gain/loss

    Key Terms

    Term Definition
    Balanced diet A diet that provides all nutrients in the correct proportions
    Energy balance The relationship between energy intake and energy expenditure
    Energy surplus When energy intake exceeds energy expenditure, leading to fat storage
    Energy deficit When energy expenditure exceeds energy intake, leading to weight loss
    Metabolism The chemical processes in the body that maintain life and require energy

    How Do Our Bodies Use Energy?

    Introduction

    Humans and other animals need energy to live. We use energy for:

    • Growth – building new cells and tissues
    • Repair – healing wounds, replacing worn-out cells
    • Movement – walking, running, breathing, even blinking
    • Keeping warm – maintaining body temperature (especially in cold environments)

    Our bodies get this energy from food.

    Measuring Energy in Food

    Units of Energy

    Unit Symbol Equivalent
    Joule J Base unit of energy
    Kilojoule kJ 1 kJ = 1000 J

    Example: The energy needed to lift an apple from the floor onto a table is about 1 J. Most foods contain much more — so we use kilojoules (kJ).

    Reading Nutrition Labels

    Nutrition labels show how much energy (and nutrients) are stored in food. They usually give values:

    • Per serving (e.g., per bun, per burger)
    • Per 100g (for easy comparison between products)

    Example Label: Falafel Burger & Bun

    Bun (65g)

    Nutrient Per bun (65g) Per 100g
    Energy 544 kJ / 130 kcal 837 kJ / 200 kcal
    Protein 6 g 9.2 g
    Carbohydrate 21 g 32.3 g
    Fat 2.5 g 3.85 g
    Fibre 1.2 g 1.8 g
    Sodium 0.2 g 0.3 g

    Falafel Burger (125g)

    Nutrient Per burger (125g) Per 100g
    Energy 1990 kJ / 476 kcal 1592 kJ / 381 kcal
    Protein 17 g 13.6 g
    Carbohydrate 63 g 50 g
    Fat 15 g 12 g
    Fibre 7 g 5.6 g
    Sodium 2 g 1.6 g

    Note: “of which sugar” or “of which saturates” refers to subcategories within carbs/fats.

    Calculation Examples

    Example 1: Energy in 100g of falafel burger

    From the label:

    Per 100g → Energy = 1592 kJ

    So, 100g of falafel burger contains 1592 kJ of energy.

    Example 2: If Mark eats two burgers meals (each meal = 1 falafel burger + 1 bun), How much total energy mark use up?

    Step 1: Find energy per full meal (burger + bun)

    • Falafel burger (125g): 1990 kJ
    • Bun (65g): 544 kJ

    → Total per meal = 1990 + 544 = 2534 kJ

    Step 2: Multiply by 2 meals

    → 2534 × 2 = 5068 kJ

    Mark consumes 5068 kJ of energy from eating two falafel burgers with buns.

    Why Do Different People Need Different Amounts of Energy?

    Your body’s energy needs depend on:

    • Age – children and teens need more for growth
    • Activity level – exercise burns more energy than watching TV
    • Body size – larger bodies need more energy to maintain
    • Gender – males often need slightly more due to muscle mass
    • Health status – illness or recovery may increase needs

    If you do a lot of exercise, you need more energy than if you spend most of your time watching television.

    Review Questions

    Question 1: Why does your body need food?

    Answer:
    Food provides energy for living processes: growing, repairing tissues, moving, and staying warm. It also supplies nutrients like protein, vitamins, and minerals essential for health.

    Question 2a: How much energy does 100g of falafel burger contain? Give your answer in kilojoules.

    Answer:
    From the nutrition label:
    → 1592 kJ

    Question 2b: Mark eats two burgers (each burger is one falafel burger in a bun). How much energy is in the food he eats?

    Answer:
    Energy per burger + bun = 1990 kJ + 544 kJ = 2534 kJ
    For two meals: 2534 × 2 = 5068 kJ

    Final Answer: 5068 kJ

    Key Takeaways

    Energy is measured in joules (J) and kilojoules (kJ); 1 kJ = 1000 J
    Food labels show energy content per serving and per 100g
    Your body uses energy for growth, repair, movement, and warmth
    Active people need more energy than sedentary people
    *Always check both components when calculating total energy (e.g., burger + bun!)

    Study Tip

    Practice reading real food labels at home! Compare snacks, drinks, or meals — calculate total energy for different portions. This helps build real-world math and science skills.

    Practice Questions

    Level 1: Basic Ratio Calculations

    Q1. Cheddar cheese contains 1120 kJ per 100g.
    Calculate the energy in: - a) 50g of cheddar cheese - b) 200g of cheddar cheese - c) 75g of cheddar cheese

    Q2. Banana contains 371 kJ per 100g.
    If Sarah eats a banana weighing 150g, how much energy does she consume?

    Q3. Peanut butter contains 3100 kJ per 100g.
    Calculate the energy in: - a) 50g of peanut butter - b) 25g of peanut butter - c) 10g of peanut butter

    Level 2: Intermediate Calculations

    Q4. A chocolate bar contains 2100 kJ per 100g.
    - a) How much energy is in a 30g chocolate bar? - b) How much energy is in a 75g chocolate bar? - c) Tom eats 1.5 chocolate bars (each bar is 50g). Calculate total energy consumed.

    Q5. Orange juice contains 188 kJ per 100ml.
    Calculate the energy in: - a) 200ml of orange juice - b) 150ml of orange juice - c) 350ml of orange juice - d) A small carton containing 250ml

    Q6. Bread contains 785 kJ per 100g. Each slice weighs 40g.
    - a) Calculate the energy in one slice of bread - b) Calculate the energy in 3 slices of bread - c) Calculate the energy in a whole loaf containing 20 slices

    Level 3: Advanced Multi-Step Problems

    Q7. Emma makes a sandwich with: - 2 slices of bread (40g each) - 30g of cheddar cheese - 15g of peanut butter

    Calculate the total energy content of her sandwich.

    Q8. Jake's breakfast includes: - 1 banana (120g) - 1 slice of bread (40g) - 200ml of orange juice

    Calculate the total energy Jake consumes for breakfast.

    Q9. Compare the energy content: - a) Which has more energy: 50g of chocolate or 100g of banana? - b) How much more energy does it contain?

    Q10. A snack box contains: - 1 apple (150g) - 20g of cheddar cheese - 10g of peanut butter

    • a) Calculate the total energy in the snack box
    • b) If you wanted to reduce the energy by 200 kJ, which item would you remove or reduce?

    Level 4: Real-World Problems

    Q11. Daily Energy Requirements:

    Person Daily Energy Need
    Child (8-10 years) 8000 kJ
    Teenager (13-15 years) 10000 kJ
    Adult (moderate activity) 10500 kJ

    Lisa (a teenager) eats: - Breakfast: 2 slices of bread with 20g peanut butter + 200ml orange juice - Lunch: Sandwich (2 bread slices + 40g cheese) + 1 apple (120g) - Snack: 30g chocolate bar - Dinner: To be calculated

    • a) Calculate Lisa's energy intake from breakfast, lunch, and snack
    • b) How much energy can she still consume at dinner to meet her daily requirement?

    Q12. Weight Management:

    Tom wants to gain weight and needs an extra 2000 kJ per day. Which option would provide closer to 2000 kJ? - Option A: 100g cheddar cheese + 1 banana - Option B: 50g peanut butter + 1 apple - Option C: 100g chocolate + 200ml orange juice

    Show your calculations for each option.
